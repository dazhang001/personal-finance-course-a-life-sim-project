Hello!

And a (hopefully warm) welcome to what I dub "the Life Simulation Project!" (no trademark!).

Before we even get started, I'm obligated to answer this question:

If there are other great personal finance curriculums and financial literacy websites out there (which woud certainly be more polished than mine, with leading experts cosigning on), why am I even creating this in the first place?

I might not inspire too much confidence with this, but: who knows? 😝

What I do know and recognize is that there is a critical need to improve financial literacy, no matter who or where you are. No one is exempt from life's biggest ally and seemingly enemy, too: money! 💸

You might argue that personal finance is as easy as just making sure that the money coming in should be greater than or equal to the money going out. I don't think anyone can really argue against this piece of advice. If I had to put it in mathspeak, just make sure:

$$\text{Money In} \geq \text{Money Out}$$

BUT...!

It's never that easy. While that may be the first layer of defense toward achieving economic satisfaction (more on that definition later), how much does it help for the following questions you'd expect to propogate on a Google search bar : 🔎

  • How much should I put toward a vacation fund? Deposit in a savings account?
  • Which bank should I choose? What accounts should I open?
  • Should I buy a home? What's a mortgage?
  • What is my credit score, and why does it matter?
  • How does one do their taxes? (Uncle Sam wants his money!)
And, one of my favorites:
  • What do you mean...debt? I thought my credit card was free money! :(

--- --- ---

You see, not so simple!

And I'm not in the business of convincing you that it'll ever be simple. I'm of the opinion that no one can claim they are an expert at personal financial management until they are on their proverbial deathbed 💀. And that above all else, everyone has their different opinions and viewpoints about the life they want to live. So there is little use for me to push a one-size, fits-all ruleset on financial responsibility that unnecessarily constrains you 🫵—my dear student 👩‍🎓—to a life you might not want to live.

The fun thing, though, is that all of these ideas of financial literacy and responsible personal financial planning can be learned no matter what your worldview is on money or what life you're interested in living.

And it involves this not-so-little-known idea called choice.

In your life, you will choose. Even if it's an illusion, making choices and deciding is what we do every moment of the day. Personal finance is more about decision-making than anything – deciding on what you think best benefits you, both in the short- and long-term.

And, because it's impossible to have "one right answer" for your personal financial journey (and life!), the fun part is experimenting on what to do, deciding on what you want, and defending the decisions you inevitably make. That's what we do as humans. Wouldn't it make sense to do the same here?

That being said, no matter what path we take, we should still learn to act with responsibility. That much we cannot compromise on. But instead of forcing you into a fixed box, instilling behaviors fit for robots learning how to properly manage their currency equivalent (should we call them ro-bucks...?), I want us to expand that "responsibility" box through the nature of defended decision-making!

And we're gonna have some fun along the way!

Let's GO!

What Will This Class Be About?

Are we tired of learning from (and teaching, for my teachers) classes built on lectures, slide decks, and topics/units that don't connect to each other?

They have their place, I'm sure. I'd even go as far to say that they sometimes have their uses.

Well, if you came here looking for that experience, I hate to be the bearer of bad news – but you will not find that here.

This project was dreamed up and built upon a simple idea:

"Play the whole game at a junior level."

Curriculum Overview

Financial Foundations

Budgeting, goals, money mindset.

Banking

Checking, savings, interest.

Credit & Debt

Credit scores and responsible borrowing.

Investing

Stocks, bonds, ETFs, retirement.

Taxes

Paychecks, tax returns, deductions.

Insurance

Managing risk and protecting assets.
