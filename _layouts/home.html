Hello!

And a (hopefully warm) welcome to what I dub "The Life Simulation Project!" (no trademark!).

Before we even get started, I'm obligated to answer this question:

If there are other great personal finance curriculums and financial literacy websites out there (which would certainly be more polished than mine, with leading experts cosigning on), why am I even creating this in the first place?

I might not inspire too much confidence with this, but: who knows? 😝

What I do know is that no matter where you are in life or who you are, no one is exempt from life's biggest ally and seemingly enemy, too: money! 💸

You might argue that personal finance is as easy as just making sure that the money coming in should be greater than or equal to the money going out.

If I had to put it in mathspeak, just make sure:

$$\text{Money In} \geq \text{Money Out}$$

I really don't think anyone can argue against this piece of advice.

However...!

It's never that easy. While that may be the first layer of defense toward achieving economic satisfaction (more on that definition later), how much does it help for the following questions you'd expect to propogate on a Google search bar of a 22-23 year-old college graduate with their first job: 🔎

  • How much should I save toward a vacation? Deposit in a savings account?
  • Which bank should I choose? What accounts should I open?
  • Should I buy a home? What's a mortgage?
  • What is my credit score, and why does it matter?
  • How does one do their taxes? (Uncle Sam wants his money!)
And, one of my favorites:
  • What do you mean...debt? I thought my credit card was free money! :(
    • Take this excerpt from an article referenced below:

      • "While some replies scoffed at the idea that anyone could believe credit cards are 'free money,' others said this misunderstanding is more common than people think. 'No one taught me or prepared me for it,' one person admitted. 'I was racking up a bill and paying off the minimum amount and had no idea what interest was.'"

Me and you both, Charlie Brown. Me and you both...

--- --- ---

You see, not so simple!

And I'm not in the business of convincing you that it'll ever be simple. I'm of the opinion that no one can claim they are an expert at personal financial management until they are on their proverbial deathbed 💀. And that above all else, everyone has their different opinions and viewpoints about the life they want to live. So there is little use for me to push a one-size, fits-all ruleset on financial responsibility that unnecessarily constrains you 🫵—my dear student 👩‍🎓—to a life you might not want to live.

The fun thing, though, is that all of these ideas of financial literacy and responsible personal financial planning can be learned no matter what your worldview is on money or what life you're interested in living.

And it involves this not-so-little-known idea called choice.

In your life, you will choose. Making choices and deciding is what we do every moment of the day. Most choices come unconsciously, our brains wired to build habit on the most mundane of things when deciding between actions inconsequential to the game of life. Decisions where uncertainty is low, and the risk (consequence) of making said decision irrelevant to the bigger picture.

But there comes times in life (many of which are already happening now) where the decisions you make come under information that is incomplete or assymetric, and/or where uncertainty plagues every corner of the process. Decisions with irreversible consequences, forcing stress on your risk tolerance. A test of your ability to evaluate alternative courses of action and the associated costs of forgoing one action for another. This concept is fundamental in your personal financial journey. You will be forced to make decisions that are consequential and that cannot be changed. Where the choices aren't obvious. Where the payoff is uncertain. And will reshape your life in ways you can't yet imagine.

💡
The actions you make regarding your finances will be the times in life where the decision-making framework you have will be tested at its finest.

And, because it's impossible to have "one right answer" for your personal financial journey (and life!), the fun part is experimenting on what to do, deciding on what you want, and defending the decisions you inevitably make. That's what we do as humans.

Wouldn't it make sense to do the same here?

That being said, no matter what path we take, we should still learn to act with responsibility. I won't lie to your face and say that drowning yourself in debt is a good idea even if you were to believe it so and defend it with whatever arguments you could muster. There are arguments, and then sometimes, there's straight ignorance. ❌

But instead of forcing you into a fixed box, instilling behaviors fit for robots learning how to properly manage their currency equivalent (should we call them ro-bucks...?), I want us to expand that "responsibility" box through the nature of defended decision-making.

You will have options, you will choose, you will be presented with recommendations, but it's up to you to decide on how you want to live your life.

Both in this hastened simulation of life we'll go together over the course of this semester...

...and inevitably in your real life, too.

And we're gonna have some fun along the way!
